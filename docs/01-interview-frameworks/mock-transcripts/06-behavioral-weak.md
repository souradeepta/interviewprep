# Mock Behavioral Interview — Weak STAR Answer

**Question:** Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager.
**Amazon LP:** Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit
**Level:** L5 (senior, applying)

---

## Transcript

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager and what happened.

**Candidate:** Sure, yeah. So, this was at my previous company. Let me give you some background first. We were a team of about twelve engineers, and we were going through a pretty big transition because the company had just been acquired and the new parent company had different processes than we were used to. Our manager was someone who had come over from the acquiring company, so he had a different background than most of us on the team. We had been working together for maybe six months at the point when this happened, so we were still kind of in the forming phase as a team...

> **[Annotation: Two-Minute Preamble]:** The candidate is two paragraphs in and hasn't said what the disagreement was about. This is the most common failure mode in behavioral interviews: over-contextualization. The interviewer needs to hear the core conflict within 30 seconds. All of the background about team size, acquisition, and forming/storming dynamics is irrelevant noise. Strong candidates put the decision and the disagreement in the first sentence.

**Candidate:** ...so anyway, my manager wanted us to do weekly status reports — like, written status emails sent out every Friday. And we had been doing async updates on Slack, which most of the team preferred. I didn't agree with the status email approach. I thought it was adding process overhead without much benefit because everyone was already sharing updates in Slack.

> **[Annotation: Soft, Process-Level Disagreement]:** The disagreement is about communication format — status emails vs. Slack. This is not a technical decision, a business decision, or anything with quantifiable stakes. "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit" is meant to be demonstrated through a decision where you had real conviction backed by data or expertise. A preference for Slack over email reads as a personal preference, not a principled professional disagreement. The interviewer is now skeptical this will score on the target LP.

**Interviewer:** How did you handle it?

**Candidate:** So I mentioned to my manager that I thought the team preferred Slack and that status emails felt redundant. And I brought it up in our team retrospective too. We kind of went back and forth on it. I felt like we had a good point — the Slack updates were already there, people were engaged with them, and adding the email felt like process for the sake of process.

> **[Annotation: No Data, No Documentation]:** The candidate "mentioned" a preference and "brought it up" in retro. There's no memo, no data, no user research, no effort to understand the manager's underlying goal (e.g., maybe the emails were for a VP stakeholder, not just the team). Strong disagreement is specific and evidenced: "I pulled our Slack channel analytics and showed that 100% of the team read updates within 2 hours, vs. email open rates of 40%." Vague advocacy scores near zero.

**Candidate:** Eventually my manager said we'd try it for a month and then revisit. So we did the status emails for a month. Honestly, it was kind of annoying but it wasn't the end of the world. We sort of forgot to revisit it and we just kept doing the emails after that.

> **[Annotation: No "Commit" — Passive Compliance]:** "It was kind of annoying but not the end of the world" and "we sort of forgot to revisit it" describe passive acceptance, not genuine commitment. "Disagree and Commit" means: after the decision is made, you fully commit — you make the new approach succeed, you don't complain about it, and you might even advocate for others to embrace it. "Sort of forgot" signals that the candidate didn't take the outcome seriously. Interviewers will score this as below the bar on the Commit dimension.

**Interviewer:** What was the impact?

**Candidate:** I mean, things were... fine? We kept doing the emails. The team wasn't thrilled about it but we adapted. I think over time we started using a template which made it faster. So I guess we made the best of it.

> **[Annotation: Vague, Unmeasured Impact]:** "Things were fine" and "we made the best of it" communicate nothing. Compare to the strong candidate's "12% P99 improvement, three engineer-weeks saved." Even for a process disagreement, the candidate could have said: "We tracked it — the emails took each engineer 15 minutes per week, totaling 3 engineer-hours per week across the team. When we eventually revisited it, I showed that data and we moved to a monthly format, saving 2.5 hours/week." No such effort was made.

**Interviewer:** Would you say you were right?

**Candidate:** Yeah, I think we were right. Slack was clearly the better tool for the kind of updates we were doing. The emails added overhead without adding value. But I guess my manager had his reasons.

> **[Annotation: Doesn't Model the Other Side]:** "I think we were right" with "I guess my manager had his reasons" is a red flag. A strong candidate would demonstrate that they genuinely tried to understand the manager's perspective — perhaps the emails were for a leadership audience outside the team, or the manager came from a culture where written status was the norm and had specific reasons. Modeling the other side shows intellectual humility and empathy; dismissing it with "I guess they had their reasons" signals the candidate is conflict-avoidant but not genuinely collaborative.

**Interviewer:** Is there another example you can think of — maybe a more technical disagreement?

**Candidate:** Hmm, let me think... there was a time we disagreed about what library to use for our frontend state management. We wanted to use Redux Toolkit and my manager preferred the Context API. We ended up going with Context API. I think Redux Toolkit would have been better for the scale we were at but we went with what the manager said.

> **[Annotation: Second Example Is Also Soft]:** When the interviewer explicitly asks for a more technical example, the candidate offers a frontend library preference disagreement with no data, no code, no production incident driving the concern, and no resolution beyond "went with what the manager said." This is the same pattern: personal preference, no data, no commit. Two weak examples in one interview is a strong signal against the LP.

**Interviewer:** What would you do differently?

**Candidate:** I think I could have pushed harder. Or maybe been more convincing. I'm not sure if it was worth the conflict.

> **[Annotation: Reflection Undermines the LP]:** "Not sure if it was worth the conflict" is a direct contradiction of "Have Backbone." The LP is precisely about knowing when it IS worth raising a concern and doing so professionally. Framing disagreement as "conflict" to be avoided signals that this candidate will self-censor when they have real concerns — which is one of the most damaging behaviors for a senior engineer operating in a fast-moving organization.

---

## Summary of Weak Signals

- Spent over two minutes on context before naming the disagreement
- Chose a low-stakes process disagreement (email vs. Slack) rather than a technical or business decision
- Presented no data, no metrics, and no written documentation to support the position
- Resolution was passive ("we sort of forgot to revisit it") rather than genuine commitment
- Impact was vague ("things were fine") with no attempt to quantify outcomes
- Used "we" throughout — no clear ownership of the position or the resolution
- Second example was equally soft (library preference) with identical pattern
- Self-reflection framed disagreement as "conflict" — directly contradicts the target LP

## What This Answer Would Score

| LP Bar | Score |
|--------|-------|
| 1 — Below bar | ❌ Does not demonstrate the LP |
| 2 — Meets bar (L3-L4) | ❌ Low-stakes example, no data, no commit |
| 3 — Exceeds bar (L5) | ❌ Would need a substantively different story |
| 4 — Exceptional (L6) | ❌ — |

## What the Candidate Should Have Done Differently

1. **Choose a substantive example:** A technical decision with real consequences — architecture, infrastructure, product direction — not a communication format preference.

2. **Lead with the conflict:** "I disagreed with my manager about migrating to a microservices architecture before we had the operational maturity to support it." Name the tension in sentence one.

3. **Back it with data:** Pull metrics, model the risk, write a memo. Show the work.

4. **Model the other side:** "My manager's concern was X, which I took seriously. My counter was Y." Demonstrating you understand the opposing view is a prerequisite to disagreeing credibly.

5. **Show the commit:** Even if partially overruled, execute fully. "Once the decision was made, I ran the migration myself and made sure it succeeded" is worth more than "I thought I was right."

6. **Quantify the outcome:** Whatever happened — it worked, it didn't, we learned — put a number on it. Numbers make stories stick.
